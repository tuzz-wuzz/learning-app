# Kindergarten Learning Activities & Games

This guide provides interactive activities and games to support the Kindergarten curriculum. Each activity includes differentiation options and inclusive strategies for students with learning disabilities.

---

## Activity 1: The Mystery Bag
**Subjects:** Science (Senses), English (Descriptive Language), Math (Shapes)

### Description
Place an object inside a non-transparent bag. Students reach in without looking and use their sense of touch to describe the object's properties (size, texture, shape) before guessing what it is.

### Differentiation
- **Make it Easier:** Use very familiar objects (a spoon, a ball, a block). Provide a "Word Bank" of descriptive words with pictures (e.g., fuzzy, hard, round).
- **Make it Harder:** Use complex objects with multiple parts. Ask students to draw what they feel before pulling it out.

### Inclusive Strategies
- **For Dyslexia/Language Processing:** Use "Touch and Talk" cards. Instead of naming the object, the student can point to a picture of the object or its property.
- **For Sensory Sensitivities:** Allow students to see the object first if they are anxious about touching unknown things, or use a "peek" window in the bag.

## Activity 2: Number Line Jumps
**Subjects:** Math (Counting, Addition/Subtraction), PE (Gross Motor)

### Description
Create a large number line on the floor (0–10 or 0–20) using tape. Call out a number for students to jump to, or give simple addition/subtraction problems (e.g., "Start at 2 and jump forward 3").

### Differentiation
- **Make it Easier:** Focus on simple recognition (0–5). Use physical number cards they can hold while they jump.
- **Make it Harder:** Introduce "counting back" for subtraction. Ask them to jump by 2s or 10s.

### Inclusive Strategies
- **For Dyscalculia:** Provide a hand-held number line for the student to follow along with their finger while they jump to reinforce the visual-spatial connection.
- **For Physical Disabilities:** Replace jumping with moving a token on a desktop number line or "driving" a wheelchair to the marked lines.

## Activity 3: Shaving Cream Writing
**Subjects:** English (Phonics, Letter Formation), Art

### Description
Spread a thin layer of shaving cream (or sand/salt) on a tray. Students use their pointer finger to "write" letters, sight words, or numbers.

### Differentiation
- **Make it Easier:** Place a large printed letter card under a clear tray so they can trace the letter in the cream.
- **Make it Harder:** Call out a sound (e.g., "/b/") and have them write the letter, or dictate CVC words (e.g., "cat").

### Inclusive Strategies
- **For Dysgraphia:** This is a key "multisensory" intervention. The lack of resistance from a pencil helps students focus on the *shape* rather than the *grip*. Use a "top-down" verbal cue for each letter (e.g., "Start at the top, big line down").
- **For Sensory Avoidance:** Place the shaving cream inside a sealed Ziploc bag so the student can trace on the outside without getting their hands messy.

## Activity 4: Habitat Sort & Match
**Subjects:** Science (Animals/Habitats), Geography

### Description
Provide pictures of animals and pictures of different habitats (ocean, forest, desert). Students work in pairs to place the animals in their correct "homes."

### Differentiation
- **Make it Easier:** Sort only two distinct habitats (e.g., Water vs. Land).
- **Make it Harder:** Discuss *why* an animal lives there (e.g., "The polar bear has thick fur for the cold").

### Inclusive Strategies
- **For ADHD:** Turn it into a "Scavenger Hunt." Hide animal pictures around the room and have students "rescue" them and bring them back to the correct habitat station.
- **For Visual Impairment:** Use toy animal figures and textured habitat mats (e.g., sand for desert, blue silk for ocean, grass/leaves for forest).

## Activity 5: Story Retelling with Puppets
**Subjects:** English (Comprehension, Speaking & Listening)

### Description
After a read-aloud, students use finger puppets or popsicle stick characters to act out the beginning, middle, and end of the story.

### Differentiation
- **Make it Easier:** Focus on just naming the characters and one thing that happened.
- **Make it Harder:** Ask the student to change the ending of the story or add a new character.

### Inclusive Strategies
- **For Autism (ASD):** Use "Social Scripts" or "Story Starters" to help prompt the dialogue. Use "First/Then" boards to structure the retelling.
- **For Selective Mutism:** Allow the student to use the puppets to "act out" the story silently or with a partner they feel comfortable with, without requiring them to speak to the whole class.

## General Strategies for Learning Disabilities

### For Dyslexia (Reading/Phonological Processing)
- **Elkonin Boxes:** Use "sound boxes" where students push a token into a box for every sound they hear in a word.
- **Color Coding:** Use different colors for vowels and consonants.
- **Audio Support:** Use audiobooks or text-to-speech tools alongside physical books.

### For Dysgraphia (Writing/Fine Motor)
- **Alternative Tools:** Offer triangular pencil grips, weighted pens, or slant boards.
- **Oral Assessment:** Allow students to dictate their "writing" to an adult or record a voice memo.
- **Highlighted Paper:** Use paper with a highlighted bottom line to help with letter placement.

### For Dyscalculia (Math/Number Sense)
- **Concrete over Abstract:** Always use physical manipulatives (blocks, beads) before moving to written numbers.
- **Subitizing Practice:** Use dice or dominos to help students "see" a number of items without counting them one by one.

### For Executive Function/ADHD
- **Visual Schedules:** Use picture icons to show the sequence of the activity.
- **Timers:** Use visual countdown timers to show how much "work time" is left.
- **Movement Breaks:** Incorporate "Brain Breaks" between focused activities.
